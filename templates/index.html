Lung cancer is a serious disease that arises when abnormal cells in the lungs begin to multiply uncontrollably, forming a tumor. It's a leading cause of cancer-related deaths worldwide.

There are primarily two types of lung cancer: non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) and small cell lung cancer (SCLC). NSCLC is the most common type, accounting for about 85% of lung cancers. It includes several subtypes, such as adenocarcinoma and squamous cell carcinoma. SCLC tends to grow and spread more rapidly.

The primary risk factor for lung cancer is smoking, both firsthand and secondhand. Other factors that can increase the risk include exposure to radon gas, occupational hazards like asbestos, and air pollution.

Diagnosis of lung cancer typically involves imaging tests like chest X-rays, CT scans, and PET scans. A biopsy is often necessary to confirm the diagnosis and determine the type of cancer.

Treatment options for lung cancer vary depending on the stage, type, and overall health of the patient. Common treatments include surgery, chemotherapy, radiation therapy, targeted therapy, and immunotherapy. Early detection and prompt treatment are crucial for improving outcomes.
